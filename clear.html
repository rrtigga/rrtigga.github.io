You've heard this a million times - 2016 was a different election.

A couple things in particular annoyed me during this political cycle. The whole media was even more biased than they usually were in their coverage. Social media created filtered bubbles that caused people to only consume content that agrees with their views and beliefs which destroyed our ability to empathize with each other. And because of all this noise it's been increasingly hard to get simple, unbiased and engaging news. This is why I'm pursuing Clear to solve this.

Background

It’s 2017. We can order food with a button. We can face chat on our smartphone with people across the globe. We can order transportation in an app. We’ll have self-driving cars soon. We have all the information in the world at our fingertips.

But why is it still hard to get simple facts from the news?

We live in social media echo chambers.

You only see posts only from like-minded friends and media sources. Think about how consuming this information on a daily-bias will impact the way you think. Facebook and Twitter is home to hyper-partisan news and confirmation bias. This is your filtered and personal place where no one and nothing will dare challenge you. And if you post something on Facebook or Twitter feed, the people who agree with you will see it first and validate you.

Here’s an example of a liberal vs. conservative Facebook feed generated by WSJ graphics.

The media lies to us to push their agenda

The immediacy of internet has caused media outlets to focus on garnering clicks, views and ads rather than delivering high-quality content. It doesn’t matter if you’re right anymore. It doesn’t matter if you have facts anymore. If you’re a journalist and something happens and you report it first, you’ll get the most clicks and views — you don’t care about accuracy, you care about numbers.

“If you don’t read the news, you’re uninformed. If you read the news, you’re misinformed.”

Since all media outlets are in competition for viewership, they will target a specific audience to capture the market and present their biased version of the truth and entertainment as opposed to facts. This is why NowThis has the highest number on Facebook videos. They focus on the millennial liberal audience and post visual videos on social issues and bashing Trump; they don’t need to report the truth because that’s what liberals like to hear. On the other hand, Fox News and Breitbart caters to conservative adults across the country. How many times have you seen Fox News criticizing Obama?

Fact-check? Ain’t nobody got time for that.

Americans become so engrossed with their lives; they don’t have time to fact-check the growing amount of news they consume. They’re satisfied with their version of the truth that agree with their beliefs and values. Even when the truth hits us in the face, we quickly dismiss it and present opinions from our personal source of news. Spun versions of the truth become “facts”. We can’t separate fact from opinions anymore. We prefer an entertaining spun version of the truth because that’s easier to consume and share.

Hypothesized Problem:

  1. Social media creates echo chambers that inhibits the users’ ability to get facts and diverse sides of the news.
  2. Most news media users consume are one-sided, opinionated and biased, devoid from real facts.
  3. We have limited time to fact-check the amount of news we consume.

Process:

I'll be emphasizing a human-centered design process.

Tools:

Sketch 3, Invision, Pen+Paper, TypeForm, Google, Google Sheets, UserTesting.com

Platform:

A web prototype. Mobile news consumption has risen rapidly and the the biggest driver of news consumption is social media. Almost all users I surveyed said the primary source of news is through Facebook, Twitter, Reddit, etc.

Target Audience:

People who are 18–29 years old who are social media users and keep up with news and politics.

Defined Opportunity:

People find it difficult to get quick, unbiased, engaging news. We assume this because our surveyed users demonstrate they’re concerned with getting accurate news, but can’t separate facts from opinion/spin and bias from news. They get most their news from their social media feed and have limited time to fact-check headlines. This problem is critical to solve because a properly functioning society depends on a well-informed and educated electorate. We think people can be better informed if we provide concise, engaging and unbiased news for people to consume.

Defined Success and Roadblocks:

We will be successful if we can encourage users to read for facts, see all sides of the news and think critically to form their own views. We are constrained by the fact people spend limited time reading news, have short attention spans and already have bias, so we will have to provide quick, unbiased news in an engaging way.

User survey analysis

Here’s what I analyzed and learned from my user surveys. I compiled the main points into a spreadsheet. I organized it among the following:

  1. Context - how they got to the news
  2. Pain - what is frustrating about their news experience
  3. Behavior - how do they typically act and interact with consuming news
  4. Pleasure - what do they aim to achieve, what are users’ goals and needs

Ideate: Come up with creative solutions after learning about our users

I sketched out some wireframes and had two iterations.

  1. The first iteration included a search bar for the user as the central call-to-action for information. A lot of news websites have redundant information of the same topic cluttered in separate sections of the site. Likewise you see the similar articles in your social media feed all with different opinions. If you google “Trump refugee ban”, you receive articles from people criticizing the ban, supporting the ban, conflicting opinions, and arguments discussing the legality of the ban. This iteration gives you concise and engaging factual information regarding the ban, so you can form your opinion. Furthermore, there is a debate section to see both sides of the arguments from users who have read the information.
  2. The second iteration is a “Wikipedia for news” concept. It has the same qualities of the first iteration and you’re able to receive topics; however, users can join the community to collaborate on writing real-time news articles. There are several logistical details that need to be figured out. One is structuring a framework for an trustworthy community of collaborators to create and edit the news in real-time. If Wikipedia can organize the world to create the knowledge library of the world, this can too. Public authors on Wikipedia fixate themselves over the smallest edits and, but they have proven transparency brings not only trust, but also accurate information for the masses. It would be great to have one place on the web where the world can come together to analyze and distribute the news and not just accept the opinions we are presented from mainstream media.

Prototyping

Since my second iteration was more complex, I decided to go with my first design. I produced a high-fidelity mockup on Sketch put them on Invision.

Testing & Feedback

View full results: Click the Prototype Feedback Tab.

Key Findings

  • Most users found the content to be effective — unbiased and straight to the point
  • The excessive emoji use was detracting to quality of the writing. It can make the content seem lower-quality and appear that I’m talking down to my readers
  • Users wanted to see more sources cited and want to include where this information was coming from for reliability
  • Some users felt though this information was concise and engaging, it could be limiting and simple. There’s a reason why most news articles are so long. They wanted more context around the base of the information. Perhaps when more is added, it can be a useful daily resource for them
  • Users liked how the debate section offered different viewpoints; however, one said if the issue does not come to a definitive conclusion, it may turn into a partisan debate or like a Facebook comment section
  • Users said more social context can be given to the article. It’s great to see the travel ban from the judge update, but how about the ongoing case about legality?
  • Using slang could be ineffective towards some users and a turn-off. However, some users really loved the use of emojis because it made the article appear visually appealing, not dense and fun. They said it targeted the millennial audience well
  • All users understood the mission of this website — to provide simple facts, not biased opinions, on real-time news and topics. I was pleased
  • Users would like to see references to the media to directly address how they responded
  • Users understood the home screen page and liked the strong call-to-action with the search bar along with popular topics at the bottom, since lots of news websites are cluttered and hard to find the search bar

Next Steps

  1. I would strengthen the content of the news article and cite specific courses. I would cut the emoji use and consider tradeoffs between making the article seem relatable and millennial friendly while not compromising on quality.
  2. I would include references from media articles and provide definitive statements on whether popular circulated information is true or false. Definitiveness can boost credibility.
  3. I would like to go back to the ideation stage and test more designs with users, especially the “Wikipedia for News” idea.

I have a lot of feedback to use. I’d love to get more, break down my findings, go back to ideation and then test more designs. I may even go back to the stage 1 — empathy.
